# Starhusk — Full Game Design and Implementation Plan

## 1. Overview

**Starhusk** is a 2D pixel-art space exploration, automation, and megastructure-building game.

The player begins as a small ship in a procedurally generated star system. They fly through space with Asteroids-like movement, explore fog-covered regions, discover planets and asteroid fields, extract finite resources, build logistics pipelines, and use stellar energy from Dyson structures to power an expanding industrial network.

The main long-term goal is to build a **stellar engine** powerful enough to move beyond the starting solar system. Once the player reaches other systems, the scale expands. The player eventually discovers alien hive civilizations doing the same thing: harvesting stars, building Dyson structures, consuming resources, and expanding through the galaxy.

At that point, the game becomes both an automation game and a civilization-choice game. The player may choose friendship, treaties, and peaceful resource competition, or conflict, conquest, and territorial war.

The endgame moves toward building a galactic-scale Dyson network: a massive distributed megastructure across many star systems.

## 2. Core Game Pillars

### 2.1 Fly, Explore, Build

The player should always feel connected to the world through their ship.

The game is not only menu-based automation. The player physically flies through space, discovers things, places structures, repairs systems, follows resource routes, investigates alien stations, and personally witnesses the industrial transformation of star systems.

### 2.2 Automation With Pressure

Automation is powerful but never completely effortless.

The player’s network is pressured by:

* Finite resources
* Maintenance decay
* Energy limits
* Heat buildup
* Logistics bottlenecks
* Alien competition
* Territorial claims
* System depletion

The goal is not simply to build once and forget. The goal is to build systems that can survive, adapt, and scale.

### 2.3 Stars as Food

The central fantasy is that civilizations consume stars.

A Dyson sphere is not just an energy structure. It is a mouth around a sun.

A stellar engine is not just transportation. It is a way to drag civilization toward the next feeding ground.

The title **Starhusk** should be reflected everywhere: depleted asteroid belts, scarred planets, dim abandoned systems, broken logistics routes, and alien civilizations leaving similar husks behind.

### 2.4 Meaningful Choices, Not Passive Upgrades

The skill tree should not contain boring passive stat boosts like “+10% mining speed.”

Instead, every unlock should create a new behavior, command, structure, route type, diplomatic option, combat tactic, or way of interacting with the world.

The tree should be somewhat committal. The player can dabble early, but deeper doctrine choices should lock out or heavily restrict opposing behaviors.

### 2.5 First Contact as a Mirror

The hive aliens are not just enemies. They are a mirror of the player.

The player discovers that another species is doing the same thing: mining planets, building Dyson structures, extracting energy, and leaving used-up systems behind.

The central question becomes:

Are you building a future, or are you only leaving husks behind?

## 3. Plot and Story

## 3.1 Starting Premise

The player begins in a single star system with a small ship, basic tools, and limited knowledge of the galaxy.

Possible story setup:

Humanity, or a post-human machine civilization, has launched the player as the first autonomous stellar architect. The home civilization needs energy, expansion, survival, or escape. The player’s mission is to build the first self-sustaining Dyson infrastructure network and eventually construct a stellar engine.

The early story should be sparse. The first hours should feel lonely, quiet, and exploratory.

The player hears only:

* Ship computer messages
* Scanner discoveries
* Resource warnings
* Construction updates
* Dyson sphere milestones
* Strange anomaly signals

## 3.2 Early Game Story Arc

The player discovers their local system.

Early objectives:

1. Learn to fly.
2. Mine basic asteroids.
3. Scan nearby planets.
4. Build the first storage station.
5. Build the first resource extractor.
6. Build the first solar collector.
7. Establish a basic logistics route.
8. Begin Dyson swarm construction.
9. Build maintenance infrastructure.
10. Discover signs of prior cosmic activity.

At this stage, the game should feel like survival through construction.

## 3.3 Mid Game Story Arc

The player’s system becomes industrialized.

The star is partially surrounded by collectors. Cargo drones move between planets. Asteroids are being stripped. Planets show mining scars. Systems begin to decay if not maintained.

The player begins preparing a stellar engine.

Mid-game discoveries:

* Strange signals from deep space
* Old wreckage
* Non-human alloys
* Unusual resource depletion in nearby systems
* Anomalies that suggest the player is not alone

## 3.4 Stellar Engine Arc

The stellar engine is the first major campaign goal.

To build it, the player needs:

* Large Dyson energy output
* Rare stellar materials
* Stable logistics
* Advanced cooling
* Maintenance chains
* Gravity control components
* Huge quantities of frame materials

Once built, the stellar engine allows the player to reach a new star system.

This should feel like a major achievement, almost like launching a rocket in Factorio.

## 3.5 First Contact Arc

After expanding beyond the starting system, the player eventually finds an alien-harvested system.

First signs:

* Unknown energy grid
* Alien cargo routes
* Depleted planets
* Hive-like Dyson structures
* Workers repairing machinery
* Organic-industrial stations
* Foreign territory boundaries

The player does not first meet a king, general, or ambassador.

The player meets a worker.

This is the “first men on the moon” moment, but strange and funny. It is historically massive for the player’s civilization, but to the alien worker, the player is just blocking a work lane.

Example first contact scene:

Ship AI:
“Confirmed: intelligent extraterrestrial life.”

Hive Worker:
“Unregistered vessel, remove yourself from Refinery Lane Seven.”

Player dialogue options:

1. I come in peace.
2. What is this place?
3. Are you harvesting this star?
4. Take me to your leader.
5. This system is mine.
6. Sorry, moving now.

If the player says:

“Take me to your leader.”

The worker responds:

“Leader is an imprecise word. Do you request the Shift-Mind, the Brood Speaker, the Route Queen, the Memory Queen, or the Hunger Above All?”

## 3.6 Alien Diplomacy Arc

The player is taken to a Speaker caste.

The Speaker explains that the hive does not see star harvesting as evil. It sees it as metabolism. Systems are food. Dyson structures are mouths. Stellar engines are legs. The galaxy is a body being consumed, organized, or transformed.

The player is then offered a major relationship choice.

## 3.7 Friendship or Conflict Choice

The player can choose peaceful coexistence or territorial war.

### Friendship Path

The player signs some kind of Accord.

Friendship means:

* Peaceful resource competition
* Negotiated borders
* Shared maps
* Trade routes
* Joint megastructure projects
* Diplomatic missions
* Access to hive bio-tech
* Restrictions on mining, attacking, and expansion

This path is safer but more constrained.

The player may not freely mine hive-claimed planets, destroy alien infrastructure, or build military Dyson weapons without consequences.

### Conflict Path

The player refuses the Accord or attacks.

Conflict means:

* Territory war
* Resource seizure
* Capturing hive stations
* Destroying alien pipelines
* Military expansion
* Salvaging hive technology
* Fewer restrictions
* More danger

This path is freer but much more dangerous.

The hive begins raiding, sabotaging, blockading, and contesting systems.

## 3.8 Endgame Story Arc

The player and hive are not the only forces in the galaxy.

The endgame may reveal:

* Other civilizations also harvesting stars
* Ancient machines that prevent stellar exploitation
* A cosmic threat that notices high energy signatures
* The reason many civilizations become star-harvesters
* The possibility that the galactic Dyson network is either salvation or destruction

The final project is a galactic-scale Dyson network, possibly involving:

* Many Dyson spheres
* Interstellar energy beams
* Stellar engines
* Repositioned stars
* Galactic-core stabilization
* A huge defensive or transcendence structure

## 4. Style and Tone

## 4.1 Visual Style

Starhusk should use 2D pixel art with strong silhouettes and readable colors.

The camera is top-down or slightly angled top-down. The game takes place in open 2D space.

Visual priorities:

* Clear ship movement
* Readable planets
* Distinct resource nodes
* Satisfying logistics lines
* Glowing Dyson structures
* Beautiful but simple stars
* Fog-of-war exploration
* Pixelated but atmospheric space backgrounds
* Industrial transformation over time

The style should feel like:

* Lonely space exploration early
* Busy automation network mid-game
* Dark industrial cosmic horror late-game

## 4.2 Mood Progression

The mood should evolve.

### Early Game

Quiet, lonely, mysterious.

The player is a tiny ship in a dark system.

### Mid Game

Busy, productive, industrial.

Cargo drones fly. Stations glow. Resource routes pulse. Dyson collectors orbit the star.

### Late Game

Massive, tense, morally complicated.

Systems are depleted. Machines break down. Alien borders appear. Stars are claimed, harvested, fought over, and moved.

## 4.3 Alien Tone

The hive aliens should not be generic evil bugs.

They should feel practical, ancient, organized, and slightly unsettling.

Their first workers can be funny and bureaucratic. Their deeper philosophy should be darker.

They do not say:

“We are evil and will destroy you.”

They say things like:

“All warm things are temporary. We give them function before silence.”

Or:

“You call it depletion. We call it digestion.”

## 6. Map Design

## 6.1 Basic Map Structure

Each star system is a large 2D space map.

It contains:

* One central star
* Planet orbits
* Asteroid fields
* Resource clusters
* Moons
* Anomalies
* Wrecks
* Fog-of-war
* Buildable zones
* Hazard zones
* Later, alien territory

The player can freely fly through the entire system.

Planets are scaled for gameplay, not realism.

The map should feel big but traversable.

## 6.2 Procedural Generation

Each system should be generated from a seed.

System generation should decide:

* Star type
* Number of planets
* Planet types
* Moon count
* Asteroid belt layout
* Resource distribution
* Hazard zones
* Anomaly placement
* Wreck placement
* Alien presence
* Resource richness
* Starting location
* Strategic chokepoints

## 6.3 Star Types

Possible star types:

* Yellow star: balanced starting systems
* Red dwarf: low energy but safer
* Blue star: high energy, high radiation
* White star: rare resources, unstable hazards
* Dying star: dangerous but rich in exotic matter
* Binary system: complex navigation and energy opportunities
* Neutron remnant: late-game gravity resources

Star type affects:

* Energy output
* Dyson efficiency
* Radiation
* Heat
* Rare resources
* Alien interest
* Stellar engine difficulty

## 6.4 Planet Types

Planet types should determine resource availability.

Examples:

### Rocky Planet

Common source of ore, silicate, copper.

### Ice Moon

Source of ice, coolant materials, fuel materials.

### Carbon World

Source of carbon and organic chemistry.

### Metallic Planet

High ore and copper, low ice.

### Volcanic Planet

Radioactives, plasma-adjacent resources, high hazard.

### Gas Giant

Fuel, hydrogen, atmospheric harvesting.

### Crystal World

Lensglass, rare silicates, anomaly data.

### Dead Planet

Low basic resources, but may contain ancient ruins or exotic materials.

### Hive-Occupied Planet

Resources may be claimed, guarded, traded for, or conquered.

## 6.5 Fog of War

The system starts mostly unexplored.

The player reveals space by:

* Flying there
* Using scanner pulses
* Launching probes
* Building sensor stations
* Gaining alien star maps
* Using late-game deep scan structures

Fog should make exploration feel meaningful.

Undiscovered regions can hide:

* Rich deposits
* Hazards
* Derelicts
* Hive routes
* Rare anomalies
* Enemy stations
* Dead systems

## 6.6 Territory Design

After alien contact, systems can contain territory zones.

Territory affects what the player can do.

Zone types:

* Player-controlled
* Hive-controlled
* Neutral
* Shared
* Contested
* Protected brood zone
* Demilitarized zone
* War zone

In friendship, borders are negotiated.

In conflict, borders are claimed and fought over.

## 7. Core Mechanics

## 7.1 Ship Movement

The ship should move like a softer, more controllable version of Asteroids.

Controls:

* Rotate left/right
* Thrust forward
* Reverse thrust or brake
* Strafe optional
* Fire mining laser
* Scan
* Place marker
* Open map
* Interact/dock
* Build

The ship should drift in space, but not be frustrating.

Early movement should be simple. Later unlocks can add new behaviors like tethering, anchoring, hazard skimming, or emergency docking.

## 7.2 Mining

Early mining is manual.

The player aims at small asteroids or resource nodes and fires a mining laser.

Manual mining produces early resources.

Later, the player builds automated miners on asteroids, planets, and moons.

Mining types:

* Manual asteroid mining
* Surface extractors
* Deep-core drills
* Asteroid belt drone mining
* Gas harvesting
* Stellar plasma harvesting
* Hive-compatible organic extraction
* Aggressive strip-mining
* Preservation-based slow extraction

## 7.3 Building

The player places structures in space, orbit, or on planets.

Build placement types:

* Free-space placement
* Orbital placement around planets
* Surface attachment points
* Star-orbit placement
* Route node placement
* Territory beacon placement
* Dyson orbit placement

Structures require resources and energy.

The player should be able to place a blueprint even if resources are missing. The automation network then delivers the required resources.

## 7.4 Energy

Energy is central.

Sources:

* Basic solar collectors
* Dyson sails
* Dyson mirrors
* Dyson shell segments
* Backup reactors
* Plasma collectors
* Alien bio-reactors
* Captured hive infrastructure

Energy is used by:

* Miners
* Refineries
* Drones
* Maintenance depots
* Research stations
* Energy relays
* Shields
* Weapons
* Stellar engine systems

Important energy concepts:

* Production
* Distribution
* Storage
* Beam routing
* Overdraw
* Heat
* Blackouts
* Priority systems

## 7.5 Heat

Dyson structures and high-energy systems generate heat.

Heat creates pressure.

If heat is ignored:

* Energy output drops
* Structures decay faster
* Collectors fail
* Maintenance demand rises
* Stellar engine instability increases
* Alien fauna or hive attention may increase

Heat solutions:

* Thermal sinks
* Coolant routes
* Radiator stations
* Shadow casting
* Starshade structures
* Hive larval heat sinks
* Reduced output modes

## 7.6 Maintenance

Maintenance is a primary mechanic.

Everything industrial wears down.

Wear sources:

* Time
* Heat
* Radiation
* Heavy use
* Overclocking
* Poor materials
* Alien sabotage
* Lack of spare parts
* Hazard zones

Failure consequences:

* Reduced output
* Broken route
* Energy leakage
* Drone crashes
* Refinery jams
* Blackouts
* Structure destruction
* Dyson instability

Maintenance progression:

### Early Game

The player manually repairs structures with the ship.

### Mid Game

The player builds repair drone depots and spare parts production.

### Late Game

The player manages automated maintenance routes, repair fleets, self-sealing structures, and emergency quarantine systems.

Maintenance should create decisions, not chores.

## 7.7 Finite Resources

Resources run out.

Every deposit has a quantity.

Examples:

* Asteroid cluster: 10,000 ore
* Ice moon: 80,000 ice
* Copper planet: 50,000 copper
* Crystal field: 3,000 rare silicate
* Exotic anomaly: 200 exotic matter

As resources are depleted, the world changes visually.

Resource depletion forces:

* Expansion
* Better logistics
* Recycling
* Trade
* Conflict
* Stellar engine construction
* Moving to new systems

## 7.8 Depletion and Reclamation

When systems are depleted, they become husks.

The player may:

* Abandon depleted systems
* Recycle old structures
* Convert systems into energy hubs
* Turn them into military outposts
* Use them as maintenance depots
* Salvage them later
* Preserve some resources through doctrine choices

The world should remember what the player did.

Resources should be understandable, finite, and tied to gameplay identity.

The player should not need 50 materials early.

Resources should be introduced gradually.

Resource tiers:

1. Raw resources
2. Refined resources
3. Dyson resources
4. Stellar engine resources
5. Alien resources
6. Research/data resources

## 10. Automation and Pipelines

## 10.1 Automation Identity

Starhusk should not copy ground belts from factory games exactly.

The automation identity should be space-based.

Resources move through:

* Cargo drone routes
* Orbital depots
* Planet-to-orbit launchers
* Mass drivers
* Energy beams
* Mobile refineries
* Trade convoys
* Alien shared routes
* Captured hive routes

## 10.2 Basic Pipeline Example

Early pipeline:

Ore asteroid → Storage hub → Refinery → Alloy storage → Solar collector construction.

Ice moon → Fuel processor → Drone port → Cargo network.

Silicate planet → Lensglass refinery → Solar mirror factory → Dyson launcher.

## 10.3 Route Types

### Cargo Drone Route

Basic automated movement between two stations.

Good for flexible logistics.

### Mass Driver Route

Fires large capsules between planets or stations.

Good for high-volume transport.

Requires alignment, energy, and receiving stations.

### Energy Beam Route

Transfers power from Dyson structures to receivers.

Can overheat, be blocked, redirected, or weaponized.

### Maintenance Route

Moves spare parts and repair drones through a system.

Keeps infrastructure alive.

### Trade Route

Used with hive or other factions.

Moves resources peacefully across borders.

### Military Supply Route

Used in conflict path.

Supplies defense platforms, occupation grids, and front-line systems.

## 10.4 Route Management

The player should be able to create and manage routes easily.

Route settings:

* Source
* Destination
* Resource type
* Desired amount
* Priority
* Allowed drones
* Territory permissions
* Maintenance requirement
* Auto-reroute enabled/disabled
* Emergency mode

## 10.5 Bottlenecks

Automation gameplay should create natural bottlenecks.

Examples:

* Too little ice to make coolant
* Cargo drones overloaded
* Energy route overheating
* Maintenance depots out of spare parts
* Miners depleted
* Refineries jammed
* Alien border blocks route
* War disrupts logistics
* Dyson structures produce power but grid cannot distribute it

The player solves bottlenecks with new structures, better route planning, doctrine unlocks, and expansion.

## 11. Dyson Sphere System

## 11.1 Dyson Progression

The Dyson sphere is the main energy progression system.

Stages:

1. Basic solar collectors
2. Solar sail launchers
3. Dyson swarm
4. Mirror arrays
5. Energy ring
6. Frame segments
7. Partial shell
8. Full stellar harness
9. Overcharged engine-ready network

## 11.2 Dyson Construction

Dyson structures require:

* Mirror film
* Frame alloy
* Energy lenses
* Thermal sinks
* Orbital stabilizers
* Large energy investment
* Continuous maintenance

The player builds Dyson infrastructure around the star.

The more complete the Dyson network becomes, the more energy the player can access.

## 11.3 Dyson Risks

Dyson systems create risks:

* Heat buildup
* Energy beam failure
* Maintenance strain
* Alien attention
* Solar storms
* Overdraw instability
* Target for enemy attack
* Grid collapse

## 11.4 Dyson as Weapon

Only available through certain conflict or advanced doctrine choices.

Possible behavior unlock:

Dyson Lance.

The player can focus stellar energy into a destructive beam.

Uses:

* Destroy enemy stations
* Break shields
* Crack structures
* Force territory control

Costs:

* Huge heat
* Diplomatic consequences
* Maintenance damage
* Possible star instability

## 12. Stellar Engine System

## 12.1 Purpose

The stellar engine is the system-completion megaproject.

It allows the player to advance to another solar system and eventually expand across the galaxy.

## 12.2 Components

Required components:

* Stellar engine anchors
* Engine core
* Gravity lattice
* Energy focusing array
* Cooling systems
* Fuel infrastructure
* Dyson power link
* Stabilizer ring

## 12.3 Construction Flow

1. Research stellar engine theory.
2. Locate rare materials.
3. Build Dyson output to required threshold.
4. Build engine anchors around the star.
5. Build cooling and stabilizer systems.
6. Manufacture engine core.
7. Charge the engine.
8. Select destination.
9. Ignite stellar engine.
10. Transition to interstellar map.

## 12.4 Stellar Engine Risks

Risks:

* Energy shortages
* Cooling failure
* Anchor misalignment
* Alien attack
* Route failure
* Heat cascade
* Star instability

The stellar engine should feel like a huge engineering event, not just a button.

## 13. Doctrine Web / Skill Tree

## 13.1 Philosophy

The skill tree should be called something like:

* Doctrine Web
* Civilization Protocols
* Command Lattice
* Expansion Directives

Recommended name: **Doctrine Web**.

The Doctrine Web does not contain passive upgrades.

No nodes should be:

* +10% mining speed
* +5% power output
* +15% cargo capacity
* +20% damage

Every node should unlock a new behavior.

## 13.2 Rule for Every Unlock

Every node must add one of these:

* New action
* New structure
* New command
* New route type
* New diplomatic option
* New combat behavior
* New maintenance behavior
* New exploration behavior
* New interaction with planets, stars, resources, aliens, or territory

If a node cannot be described as a new verb, cut it.

## 13.3 Doctrine Branches

Recommended branches:

* Pilot
* Industry
* Dyson
* Maintenance
* Exploration
* Diplomacy
* Warfare
* Hive Integration
* Stellar Engineering
* Galactic Architecture

## 13.4 Pilot Unlocks

### Anchored Drift

Deploy a temporary anchor that lets the ship pivot around a point while mining, building, or fighting.

### Tether Tow

Attach a cable to small asteroids, cargo pods, or damaged drones and physically drag them.

### Emergency Dock

Dock with damaged structures and manually repair them through a direct interaction interface.

### Survey Pulse

Charge and release a directional scan that reveals hidden deposits, cloaked structures, or anomaly traces.

### Hazard Skim

Fly close to dangerous zones to collect rare particles manually.

## 13.5 Industry Unlocks

### Mass Driver Routes

Build launch cannons that fire resource capsules between planets and stations.

### Mobile Refinery

Build a refinery ship that travels between resource fields.

### Priority Routing

Mark a project or structure so logistics systems reroute resources toward it.

### Resource Locking

Reserve resources for specific projects so other systems cannot consume them.

### Route Contracts

Create repeatable logistics orders such as “move ore from A to B until storage is full.”

## 13.6 Dyson Unlocks

### Solar Sail Launching

Launch temporary solar sails that produce energy but decay over time.

### Mirror Redirection

Aim mirrors to power structures without a normal grid connection.

### Stellar Overdraw

Pull emergency energy from the Dyson network, causing heat and instability.

### Shadow Casting

Reposition Dyson panels to cool planets, stations, or contested zones.

### Star Pulse Capture

Align collectors during stellar flares to harvest plasma condensate.

### Dyson Lance

Weaponize stellar energy through a focused beam.

Likely locked behind warfare or total claim doctrine.

## 13.7 Maintenance Unlocks

### Repair Drone Depots

Build depots that send drones to repair nearby structures.

### Failure Quarantine

Automatically isolate a broken station or route so the failure does not cascade.

### Cannibalize Structure

Dismantle one structure to repair another nearby structure immediately.

### Maintenance Routes

Create dedicated spare-parts routes.

### Patch Foam

Deploy temporary repairs that keep a structure running until proper repair arrives.

### Black Box Diagnostics

After a failure, the player can inspect the cause and unlock better alert rules or prevention options.

## 13.8 Exploration Unlocks

### Probe Launcher

Launch autonomous probes into fog-of-war.

### Breadcrumb Beacons

Drop navigation beacons to create safe routes through unexplored or hazardous regions.

### Anomaly Sampling

Collect samples from strange objects to unlock unique research choices.

### Gravimetric Mapping

Reveal hidden mass signatures such as buried moons, rogue planets, or cloaked stations.

### System Forecasting

Preview vague traits of future systems before jumping.

### Deep Route Plotting

Choose unusual jump paths to avoid hive territory or reach rare stars.

## 13.9 Diplomacy Unlocks

### Translation Matrix

Unlock meaningful dialogue with hive workers, speakers, and border officials.

### Scent-Code Passport

Travel through some hive territory without being blocked or attacked.

### Shared Claim Negotiation

Divide a system by planet, orbit, resource, or power share.

### Trade Convoys

Create automated trade routes with hive stations.

### Joint Construction

Build special projects requiring both human and hive materials.

### Border Arbitration

Resolve contested claims through concessions instead of combat.

### Worker Aid Request

Temporarily borrow hive workers to repair or stabilize a failing structure.

## 13.10 Warfare Unlocks

### Territory Claim Beacon

Deploy a beacon that contests an area.

### Boarding Drone Swarm

Capture disabled hive stations instead of destroying them.

### Route Interdiction

Place ambush satellites on enemy cargo lanes.

### Sabotage Probe

Disable an enemy relay, depot, or shield.

### Scorched Orbit

Destroy a resource site to deny it to both sides.

### Forced Salvage

Strip materials from enemy Dyson structures.

### Occupation Grid

Hold captured systems with military infrastructure.

## 13.11 Hive Integration Unlocks

Hive integration depends on friendship or conflict.

### Peaceful Hive Integration

#### Living Conduits

Grow hive-resin routes that self-repair but require brood gel.

#### Brood Repair Swarm

Host allied hive workers inside human stations for maintenance.

#### Choral Routing

Coordinate human and hive cargo systems.

#### Organic Refinery

Digest low-grade ore into usable material with little waste.

#### Larval Heat Sinks

Use living coolant organisms to absorb heat.

#### Hive Embassy Node

Build a station for advanced negotiations and shared projects.

### Hostile Hive Exploitation

#### Captured Scent Keys

Disguise drones as authorized hive traffic for short periods.

#### Chitin Plating

Add alien armor modules to stations.

#### Brood Jammer

Disrupt local hive coordination.

#### Resin Parasite

Plant a device that clogs and decays hive structures.

#### Bio-Core Reactor

Use captured organic cores for unstable power.

#### False Queen Signal

Redirect enemy workers or patrols away from a target.

## 13.12 Major Committal Choices

The Doctrine Web should include major gates.

### Industrial Mandate vs. Stewardship Protocol

#### Industrial Mandate Unlocks

* Strip-Mine Order
* Factory Queue Authority
* Emergency Labor Mode

This path emphasizes rapid extraction and construction.

#### Stewardship Protocol Unlocks

* Preserve Deposit
* Reclamation Zones
* Protected Orbit

This path emphasizes longevity, lower waste, and controlled expansion.

### Accord Path vs. Total Claim Doctrine

#### Accord Path Unlocks

* Shared Borders
* Mutual Rescue
* Joint Stellar Engine
* Peaceful hive trade

This path strengthens friendship but restricts aggression.

#### Total Claim Doctrine Unlocks

* First Strike
* Occupation Grid
* Forced Salvage
* Unrestricted seizure

This path strengthens war but damages diplomacy.

### Solar Dominion vs. Silent Star

#### Solar Dominion Unlocks

* High-output Dyson overdraw
* Broadcast energy projects
* Solar flare harvesting
* Aggressive stellar use

This path attracts attention and heat.

#### Silent Star Unlocks

* Reduced signature operations
* Hidden route construction
* Lower-profile Dyson behavior
* Avoidance of alien escalation

This path sacrifices raw output for stealth and safety.

## 20. Key Risks

## 20.1 Scope Creep

This game can become enormous.

Protect the project by building the core loop first.

Do not start with the galaxy map. Start with one system.

## 20.2 Automation Complexity

Routes can become hard to manage.

The UI must make route creation simple.

The player should understand:

* Where resources come from
* Where they are going
* Why something is blocked
* What is missing
* What is failing

## 20.3 Maintenance Annoyance

Maintenance can become irritating if too random.

Failures should be predictable, visible, and solvable.

The player should get warnings before collapse.

## 20.4 Skill Tree Bloat

The Doctrine Web should stay smaller and more meaningful.

Every node must unlock a new behavior.

Cut passive improvements.

## 20.5 Alien Timing

Aliens should not appear too early.

The player should first understand their own industrial expansion. Then aliens appear as a mirror.

The reveal works best after the player has already depleted or heavily exploited at least one system.

## 22. Final Game Identity

Starhusk is a game about becoming powerful enough to consume stars, then meeting something else already doing it.

It begins as a lonely 2D space exploration game.

It becomes a logistics and automation game.

It evolves into a Dyson sphere construction game.

It complicates itself with maintenance, depletion, and consequence.

Then it becomes a first-contact game.

Then it becomes a diplomacy or war game.

Finally, it becomes a galactic-scale megastructure game about what civilizations are willing to do to survive, expand, and feed.

The player’s machines do not simply build a future.

They leave husks behind.
